### 1.2 Key Observations About Competition Timing

- **Math Kangaroo** is notably generous with time (~3 min/question) and is specifically
  designed to be accessible and enjoyable, not stressful. Questions are arranged in
  3-point, 4-point, and 5-point tiers, with harder questions implicitly needing more time.
- **AMC 8** is faster-paced (1.6 min/question) but targets more advanced students.
- **SOF IMO** is the most time-pressured (~1.2-1.7 min/question), though many questions
  are straightforward multiple-choice that reward quick recognition.
- Competition time is NOT practice time. Competitions deliberately use time pressure as
  a differentiator. Practice should allow MORE time than competition format.

### 1.3 Recommended Time Per Question for PRACTICE (not competition)

| Grade Level | Age | Practice Time/Question | Competition Time/Question | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Grades 1-2 | 6-7 | **3-5 minutes** | ~3 min (Math Kangaroo) | Young children need processing time; working memory is still developing |
| Grades 3-4 | 8-9 | **2-4 minutes** | ~2-3 min | Faster processing but still building fluency |
| Grades 5-6 | 10-11 | **2-3 minutes** | ~1.5-2.5 min | Can handle moderate time pressure |
| Grades 7-8 | 12-14 | **1.5-3 minutes** | ~1.2-2.5 min | Approaching adult-like processing speed |

## 2. COGNITIVE DEVELOPMENT AND TIMED TESTING

### 2.1 The Math Anxiety-Timer Connection

**Jo Boaler's Research (Stanford, 2014-2022)**
- Boaler's widely cited research argues that timed tests are a significant source of
  early math anxiety, particularly for girls and students from lower socioeconomic backgrounds.
- "Timed tests cause the earliest onset of math anxiety" — Boaler, J. (2014).
  "Research Suggests Timed Tests Cause Math Anxiety." Teaching Children Mathematics.
- Her research at youcubed.org shows that math anxiety activates fear centers in the
  brain (amygdala), which literally blocks access to working memory needed for math.

**Sian Beilock's Research (University of Chicago, 2010-2015)**
- Beilock, S.L. (2008). "Math performance in stressful situations." Current Directions
  in Psychological Science.
- Demonstrated that performance pressure consumes working memory resources ("choking
  under pressure"), and this effect is STRONGER in students with HIGH working memory
  capacity — meaning your best students may be most hurt by time pressure.
- Children ages 6-8 are particularly vulnerable because their working memory capacity
  is still developing (about 2-3 items vs. 7 items for adults).

**Ramirez et al. (2013)**
- "Math Anxiety, Working Memory, and Math Achievement in Early Elementary School."
  Journal of Cognition and Development, 14(2), 187-202.
- Found that math anxiety has measurable negative effects on math performance as early
  as first and second grade (ages 6-7).
- Students with higher working memory showed LARGER negative effects from anxiety.

### 2.2 The Counter-Argument: Fluency Requires Speed

**Royer et al. (1999)**
- "Math-Fact Retrieval as the Cognitive Mechanism Underlying Gender Differences in Math."
  Contemporary Educational Psychology.
- Argues that automaticity in basic math facts IS important and timed practice builds this.
- The key distinction: **timed practice of known facts** (building fluency) vs.
  **timed testing of new concepts** (causing anxiety).

**National Mathematics Advisory Panel (2008)**
- U.S. Department of Education report concluded that "computational fluency" — including
  speed — is a legitimate and important educational goal.
- Recommended that students develop "automatic recall" of basic facts.

### 2.3 Synthesis: What the Research Actually Recommends

The consensus position from educational psychology:

1. **Ages 6-7 (Grades 1-2)**: Timers should be INVISIBLE or used as information only
   (not as pressure). Focus on encouraging problem-solving process, not speed. If you
   must time, use a generous countdown or a "beat your own time" approach rather than
   a visible ticking clock.

2. **Ages 8-9 (Grades 3-4)**: Gentle time awareness is acceptable. A progress bar or
   subtle timer is better than a prominent countdown. Allow time extensions freely.

3. **Ages 10-11 (Grades 5-6)**: Students can handle visible timers if framed positively
   ("challenge mode" vs. "you're running out of time"). Competition-style timing is
   appropriate for students who opt in.

4. **Ages 12-14 (Grades 7-8)**: Can handle competition-realistic timing. However,
   always offer an untimed practice mode. Research shows even adolescents benefit from
   low-pressure practice environments.

**Critical design principle**: Separate PRACTICE (untimed or generous timing) from
CHALLENGE/COMPETITION (realistic timing). Never make time pressure the default for
first exposure to a concept.

- Beilock, S.L. & Willingham, D.T. (2014). "Math Anxiety: Can Teachers Help Students

## 3. BLOOM'S TAXONOMY AND TIME EXPECTATIONS

### 3.1 Time by Cognitive Level

Bloom's Taxonomy defines six cognitive levels. Research on exam timing consistently shows
that higher-order questions require disproportionately more time:

| Bloom's Level | Description | Relative Time Factor | Example in Math Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Remember** | Recall facts | 1.0x (baseline) | "What is 7 x 8?" |
| **Understand** | Explain concepts | 1.3-1.5x | "Which number sentence shows 3 groups of 4?" |
| **Apply** | Use in new situation | 1.5-2.0x | "A train travels at 60 km/h. How far in 2.5 hours?" |
| **Analyze** | Break down, compare | 2.0-3.0x | "Look at the pattern. What's the rule?" |
| **Evaluate** | Judge, justify | 2.5-3.5x | "Which strategy is most efficient? Why?" |
| **Create** | Design, construct | 3.0-5.0x | "Design a shape with perimeter 20 and maximum area" |

### 3.2 Research on Time Allocation

**Furst (1981)**
- "Bloom's Taxonomy: Philosophical and Educational Issues." — One of the original
  analyses of how Bloom's levels map to assessment time.
- Recommended that test designers allocate time proportional to cognitive demand.

**Martinez (1999)**
- "Cognition and the Question of Test Item Format." Educational Psychologist, 34(4).
- Found that constructed-response questions at higher Bloom's levels take 3-5x longer
  than recognition (multiple choice) questions at the Remember level.

**Implications for LearnSmarter:**
- If your "Remember" questions get 1.5 minutes, your "Analyze" questions should get
  3-4.5 minutes.
- Tag each question with its Bloom's level and adjust the timer accordingly.
- For competition prep: students should eventually solve all levels within competition
  time, but PRACTICE should start with generous time at higher levels.

### 3.3 Practical Time Multipliers for LearnSmarter

| Question Difficulty | Grades 1-2 | Grades 3-4 | Grades 5-6 | Grades 7-8 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Easy (Remember/Understand) | 2-3 min | 1.5-2 min | 1-1.5 min | 0.75-1.5 min |
| Medium (Apply/Analyze) | 3-5 min | 2-3.5 min | 1.5-2.5 min | 1.5-2.5 min |
| Hard (Evaluate/Create) | 4-6 min | 3-5 min | 2-4 min | 2-3.5 min |

**Source references:**
- Anderson, L.W. & Krathwohl, D.R. (2001). A Taxonomy for Learning, Teaching, and
  Assessing. Pearson.
- Martinez, M.E. (1999). "Cognition and the Question of Test Item Format." Educational
  Psychologist, 34(4), 207-218.
- Furst, E.J. (1981). "Bloom's Taxonomy of Educational Objectives for the Cognitive
  Domain: Philosophical and Educational Issues." Review of Educational Research, 51(4).

## 4. HINT AND SCAFFOLD RESEARCH

### 4.1 Hints vs. Direct Answers

**Kalyuga et al. (2003) — Expertise Reversal Effect**
- "The Expertise Reversal Effect." Educational Psychologist, 38(1), 23-31.
- Key finding: scaffolding that helps beginners can actually HURT advanced learners.
- Implication: Hint systems should adapt to student proficiency. Beginners need more
  detailed hints; advanced students benefit from minimal nudges.

**Hume et al. (1996) — AutoTutor Research**
- Research on intelligent tutoring systems showed that hints that require the student
  to generate the answer (even partially) produce better learning than hints that
  reveal the answer.
- "Productive failure" — struggling and failing before receiving help leads to deeper
  learning than receiving help upfront.

**Kapur (2008, 2012) — Productive Failure**
- Kapur, M. (2008). "Productive failure." Cognition and Instruction, 26(3), 379-424.
- Kapur, M. (2012). "Productive failure in learning the concept of variance."
  Instructional Science, 40(4), 651-672.
- Students who struggled with problems BEFORE receiving instruction outperformed students
  who received instruction first, on both procedural and conceptual measures.
- This argues for: let students attempt → fail → then offer hints, rather than
  preemptive scaffolding.

### 4.2 Progressive Scaffolding (Step-by-Step Hints)

**Wood, Bruner & Ross (1976) — Original Scaffolding Theory**
- "The Role of Tutoring in Problem Solving." Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry.
- Defined scaffolding as temporary support that is gradually removed ("fading").
- The most effective tutoring provides the MINIMUM help needed for the student to
  take the next step.

**Renkl (2002) — Worked Examples and Fading**
- "Worked-Out Examples: Instructional Explanations Support Learning by Self-Explanations."
  Learning and Instruction, 12(5), 529-556.
- Faded worked examples (progressively removing steps for the student to fill in) were
  more effective than complete worked examples or no examples.
- Optimal sequence: Full worked example → Partially worked example → Problem with hints
  → Independent problem.

**VanLehn et al. (2005) — Hint Timing**
- "The Behavior of Tutoring Systems." International Journal of AI in Education.
- Found that the most effective moment to offer a hint is AFTER the student has made
  an attempt and gotten stuck (not immediately, not after too long).
- "Bottom-out hints" (just giving the answer) were negatively correlated with learning.

### 4.3 Optimal Hint Strategy for LearnSmarter

Based on the research, the recommended progressive hint system:

```
Attempt 1: Student tries on their own
  → If wrong: "Not quite! Try thinking about [general concept area]."
     (Metacognitive prompt — no content given away)

Attempt 2: Student tries again
  → If wrong: "Here's a hint: [First step of the solution approach]"
     (Process hint — reveals strategy, not answer)

Attempt 3: Student tries again
  → If wrong: "Let's break it down: [Worked example of similar problem]"
     (Worked example — shows analogous solution)

Attempt 4: If still stuck
  → Show full solution with explanation
     (Bottom-out — but with pedagogical explanation, not just the answer)
```

**Key research-backed principles:**
1. **Delay hints** — Don't offer immediately. Wait for at least one attempt.
2. **Progressive disclosure** — Start vague, get specific.
3. **Require student generation** — Even with hints, make the student do SOMETHING.
4. **Explain, don't just reveal** — When showing the answer, explain the WHY.
5. **Adapt to level** — Beginners get hints faster; advanced students get more struggle time.

**For younger children (Grades 1-2):**
- Offer hints sooner (after 1 wrong attempt, not 2)
- Use visual hints (diagrams, manipulatives, number lines)
- Keep hint language simple and encouraging

**For older children (Grades 7-8):**
- Allow more struggle time before hints
- Hints can be more abstract ("What theorem applies here?")
- Encourage students to request hints rather than auto-offering

**Source references:**
- Kapur, M. (2012). "Productive failure in learning the concept of variance."
  Instructional Science, 40, 651-672.
- VanLehn, K. (2006). "The Behavior of Tutoring Systems." International Journal of
  Artificial Intelligence in Education, 16(3), 227-265.
- Renkl, A. (2002). "Worked-Out Examples: Instructional Explanations Support Learning
  by Self-Explanations." Learning and Instruction, 12(5), 529-556.
- Wood, D., Bruner, J.S., & Ross, G. (1976). "The Role of Tutoring in Problem Solving."
  Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry, 17(2), 89-100.
- Kalyuga, S. et al. (2003). "The Expertise Reversal Effect." Educational Psychologist,
  38(1), 23-31.

## 5. GAMIFICATION AND MOTIVATION RESEARCH FOR K-8

### 5.1 What Gamification Elements Work?

**Hamari, Koivisto & Sarsa (2014) — Meta-Analysis**
- "Does Gamification Work? A Literature Review of Empirical Studies on Gamification."
  Proceedings of the 47th Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences.
- Analyzed 24 empirical studies. Found gamification generally produces positive effects
  on engagement, but effects on learning outcomes are mixed.
- Most effective elements: **clear goals, immediate feedback, progress indicators**.
- Least reliably effective: **leaderboards** (can demotivate low performers).

**Dicheva et al. (2015) — Gamification in Education**
- "Gamification in Education: A Systematic Mapping Study." Educational Technology &
  Society, 18(3), 75-88.
- The most commonly used and effective gamification elements in education:
  1. **Points** — effective when tied to specific learning behaviors, not just correct answers
  2. **Badges/Achievements** — effective for goal-setting and recognition
  3. **Leaderboards** — mixed results; can motivate competitive students but discourage others
  4. **Progress bars** — consistently effective across all ages
  5. **Narrative/Story** — particularly effective for younger children (K-4)
  6. **Levels/Unlocking** — effective for creating a sense of progression

### 5.2 Points vs. Badges vs. Leaderboards

**Points:**
- Most effective when: linked to effort (not just accuracy), visible, and spendable
- Research (Deci, Koestner & Ryan, 1999): Points that feel like "controlling" rewards
  can undermine intrinsic motivation. Points should feel informational.
- Recommendation: Award points for attempting problems, for streaks, for improvement —
  not just correct answers.

**Badges:**
- Most effective when: meaningful, achievable, varied, and tied to mastery milestones
- Abramovich, Schunn & Higashi (2013): "Are badges useful in education? It depends on
  the type of badge and the expertise of the learner." Educational Technology Research
  & Development, 61, 217-232.
- Found that "participation badges" motivated novices, while "skill badges" motivated
  more advanced students.
- Recommendation: Have both types. "First Try" badges for engagement, "Mastery" badges
  for competence.

**Leaderboards:**
- Most CONTROVERSIAL element. Research is split:
- Positive: Landers & Landers (2014) found leaderboards increased time-on-task by 29%.
- Negative: Hanus & Fox (2015) found leaderboards decreased intrinsic motivation,
  satisfaction, and final exam scores in a semester-long study.
- Key moderator: **anonymity and scope**. Class-wide public leaderboards are harmful.
  Small group or friend-only leaderboards are better. Self-referential leaderboards
  ("beat your own best") are safest.
- **For ages 6-9**: AVOID competitive leaderboards. Use personal-best tracking.
- **For ages 10-14**: OPTIONAL leaderboards with opt-in, friend groups, or anonymized.

### 5.3 Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation

**Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985, 2000)**
- The gold standard framework for understanding motivation.
- Three basic psychological needs that drive intrinsic motivation:
  1. **Autonomy** — feeling of choice and control
  2. **Competence** — feeling of mastery and growth
  3. **Relatedness** — feeling of connection to others

- Extrinsic rewards (points, prizes) can UNDERMINE intrinsic motivation if they feel
  controlling ("overjustification effect"). But they can SUPPORT motivation if they
  feel informational and autonomy-supportive.

**Practical implications for LearnSmarter:**
- **Autonomy**: Let kids choose which topics to practice, difficulty level, whether to
  use timer or not. Avoid forcing linear progression.
- **Competence**: Show growth over time ("You've improved 30%!"), not just absolute scores.
  Use mastery-based progression, not time-based.
- **Relatedness**: Allow sharing achievements with parents/friends. Cooperative challenges
  alongside competitive ones.

### 5.4 What Makes Kids Come Back? (Retention Research)

**Deterding et al. (2011) — "Gamification: Toward a Definition"**
- Short-term engagement boosters (novelty effects) wear off in 2-4 weeks.
- Long-term retention requires: **meaningful progress, variable rewards, social features,
  and genuine learning value** (parents must also see value or they'll uninstall).

**Key retention drivers from educational app research:**
1. **Daily streaks** — Duolingo's research shows streaks are the #1 retention mechanism.
   But: offer "streak freezes" to reduce anxiety and prevent ragequit.
2. **Variable rewards** — Unpredictable small bonuses (mystery boxes, bonus challenges)
   keep engagement higher than predictable rewards (Eyal, 2014, Hooked model).
3. **Push notifications / reminders** — Effective but must be gentle for children's apps.
   Parents should control notification frequency.
4. **Social proof** — "1,247 students practiced today!" creates normative motivation.
5. **Near-miss psychology** — "You got 4/5! One more to earn the badge!" is extremely
   motivating (but must be used ethically with children).
6. **Parent involvement** — Apps where parents receive progress updates and can engage
   have 2-3x higher retention rates (based on industry reports from Toca Boca,
   Khan Academy Kids, and others).

### 5.5 Age-Specific Gamification Recommendations

| Element | Grades 1-2 (6-7) | Grades 3-4 (8-9) | Grades 5-6 (10-11) | Grades 7-8 (12-14) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Points | Stars/coins with visual animations | Points with spending (avatar, themes) | Points with meaningful economy | Points tied to skill levels |
| Badges | Simple, frequent, celebratory | Tiered (bronze/silver/gold) | Achievement-based, rare = valuable | Skill-specific, portfolio-building |
| Leaderboard | NO competitive boards. Personal best only | Optional class/friend boards | Opt-in leaderboards | Full competitive features |
| Narrative | Story-driven ("Help the character!") | Quest-based | Mission-based | Challenge/tournament framing |
| Timer | Hidden or very gentle | Optional, encouraging | Visible, competition-prep | Full competition mode available |
| Streaks | Simple daily check-in | Daily streaks with freezes | Streaks + challenges | Streaks + competitive goals |

## 6. PARENT ANALYTICS — WHAT DO PARENTS ACTUALLY WANT?

### 6.1 What Information Do Parents Value Most?

**Olmstead (2013) — Pew Research Center**
- "Using Technology for Parenting" — parents' primary concerns:
  1. **Is my child making progress?** (growth over time, not just snapshots)
  2. **Where does my child struggle?** (specific weak areas)
  3. **Is my child spending time productively?** (time spent vs. time wasted)
  4. **How does my child compare to expectations?** (grade-level benchmarks, not peer ranking)

**Goodall & Montgomery (2014)**
- "Parental Involvement to Parental Engagement: A Continuum." Educational Review, 66(4).
- Found that parents want to move from passive monitoring to active engagement.
- Most valued: actionable information ("Your child should practice X this week")
  vs. raw data ("Your child scored 73%").

**Key findings from EdTech parent surveys (Common Sense Media, 2019-2023):**
1. **#1 Most Wanted**: Progress over time (85%+ of parents)
2. **#2**: Specific areas of weakness / topics to work on (78%)
3. **#3**: Time spent practicing (72%)
4. **#4**: Comparison to grade-level standards (65%)
5. **#5**: Upcoming topics / what to expect next (54%)
6. **Least wanted**: Raw scores without context, comparison to other children (< 30%)

### 6.2 How Should Progress Be Communicated?

**Research-backed best practices:**

1. **Weekly summary emails/notifications** — not daily (too noisy) or monthly (too infrequent)
   - Kraft & Rogers (2015): "The Underutilized Potential of Teacher-to-Parent Communication."
     Economics of Education Review. Found that weekly personalized messages about student
     progress increased student effort by 0.34 SD.

2. **Growth framing, not deficit framing**
   - Say: "Asha improved from 60% to 78% in fractions this month!"
   - Not: "Asha got 78% in fractions (below grade level)."
   - Yeager & Dweck (2012): Growth mindset framing in parent communication increases
     student persistence.

3. **Visual dashboards over text reports**
   - Parents prefer: progress graphs, color-coded topic mastery maps, streak calendars
   - Parents dislike: long text reports, too many numbers, jargon

4. **Actionable recommendations**
   - "Based on this week's practice, we recommend 10 minutes on multiplication facts"
   - Links directly from the report to the relevant practice section

5. **Celebration of effort, not just results**
   - "Asha attempted 47 problems this week — her most ever!"
   - Research on growth mindset (Dweck, 2006) shows that praising effort over
     innate ability leads to greater persistence.

### 6.3 Parent Dashboard — Recommended Features

Based on the research, here is the priority-ordered feature list:

**Must-Have (MVP):**
1. **Progress Over Time Graph** — line chart showing accuracy trend by week/month
2. **Topic Mastery Map** — color-coded grid (red/yellow/green) showing which topics
   are mastered, in progress, or need work
3. **Weekly Activity Summary** — questions attempted, time spent, streaks maintained
4. **Weak Area Callout** — "Focus area this week: Division with remainders"

**Should-Have (V2):**
5. **Improvement Highlights** — "Biggest improvement: Geometry (+25% this month)"
6. **Practice Recommendations** — AI-generated "what to practice next"
7. **Comparison to Grade Standard** — "Your child is meeting/exceeding Grade 4 expectations
   in 8 of 10 topic areas"
8. **Session Log** — when the child practiced and for how long

**Nice-to-Have (V3):**
9. **Competition Readiness Score** — "Based on practice, your child would score in the
   top 40% of Math Kangaroo Level 3-4"
10. **Printable Practice Sheets** — for offline practice of weak areas
11. **Parent-Teacher Sharing** — export reports in standard format

### 6.4 Parent Engagement and Retention

**Key finding**: Parental engagement with an educational app is the STRONGEST predictor
of child retention (stronger than gamification features).

- **Suskind et al. (2016)**: Parent awareness of child's learning activities increases
  home learning support, which amplifies app effectiveness.
- **Recommendation**: Send parents a brief weekly "celebration message" even when the
  child isn't performing well — focus on effort and consistency.
- **COPPA compliance**: All parent features must comply with Children's Online Privacy
  Protection Act. Children under 13 cannot share data without parental consent.

## 7. SPECIFIC RECOMMENDATIONS FOR LEARNSMART

### 7.1 Timer System Design

```
TIMER ARCHITECTURE:

Grade 1-2:
  - DEFAULT: No visible timer. Track time silently in background.
  - PRACTICE MODE: Show encouraging progress (stars filling up, not time draining away)
  - CHALLENGE MODE: Gentle timer with generous limits (2x competition time)
  - COMPETITION PREP: Only when parent/child opts in. Match Math Kangaroo timing.

Grade 3-4:
  - DEFAULT: Subtle timer (progress bar, not countdown numbers)
  - PRACTICE MODE: Optional timer, child can toggle
  - CHALLENGE MODE: Visible timer with 1.5x competition time
  - COMPETITION PREP: Realistic timing with "almost there!" encouragement

Grade 5-6:
  - DEFAULT: Visible timer
  - PRACTICE MODE: Timer with pause button
  - CHALLENGE MODE: Competition timing
  - COMPETITION PREP: Strict competition timing with performance analytics

Grade 7-8:
  - DEFAULT: Full timer visibility
  - PRACTICE MODE: Timer with optional hide
  - CHALLENGE MODE: Competition timing
  - COMPETITION PREP: AMC 8 / MATHCOUNTS realistic conditions
```

### 7.2 Hint System Design

```
HINT ARCHITECTURE:

Trigger: After 1st wrong answer (Grades 1-2) or 2nd wrong answer (Grades 3-8)

Level 1 Hint: Metacognitive prompt
  "Think about what operation to use here."
  Cost: Free (no point deduction)

Level 2 Hint: Strategy hint
  "Try breaking this into smaller parts. What's 12 x 3 if you split 12 into 10 + 2?"
  Cost: 25% point reduction for this question

Level 3 Hint: Worked similar example
  "Here's how to solve a similar problem: [worked example]"
  Cost: 50% point reduction

Level 4: Full solution
  "Here's the answer with a full explanation."
  Cost: No points for this question, but it counts as "reviewed"

Adaptive adjustment:
  - If student uses hints on >50% of problems in a topic: reduce difficulty
  - If student never uses hints and gets >90% correct: increase difficulty
  - Track "hint dependency" — flag if a student always takes hints without trying
```

### 7.3 Gamification System Design

```
GAMIFICATION ARCHITECTURE:

Points ("Brain Stars"):
  - Correct answer: 10 points
  - Correct on first try: bonus 5 points
  - Correct after hint: 5 points (reduced)
  - Attempting a hard question: 3 points (effort reward)
  - Daily login: 5 points
  - Completing a topic set: 20 points
  - Personal best time: 10 points

Badges:
  Effort badges: "First Step" (1st question), "Persistent" (10 in a row),
                 "Daily Learner" (7-day streak)
  Skill badges: "Addition Master" (90%+ in addition), "Fraction Expert",
                "Geometry Guru"
  Special badges: "Speed Demon" (under competition time), "Perfect Score",
                  "Come-Back Kid" (improved from <50% to >80%)

Streaks:
  - Daily streak counter with "streak freeze" (1 free per week)
  - Weekly goal: "Complete 5 practice sessions" (adjustable)
  - Monthly challenge: themed (e.g., "Geometry Month")

Progression:
  - Levels within each topic (Beginner → Intermediate → Advanced → Expert)
  - Visual unlock system (new avatar items, themes)
  - "Competition Ready" badge when consistently performing at competition speed

NO global leaderboards for Grades 1-4.
OPTIONAL friend leaderboards for Grades 5-8.
ALWAYS show personal improvement metrics prominently.
```
